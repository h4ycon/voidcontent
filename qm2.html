Quantum mechanics. Part 2

Okay, let's try and continue learning more about quantum mechanics.

So what is quantum mechanics really?

Today we are going to be touching upon some of the principles that dictate how these subatomic particles interact. We are going to look at the wave particle duality principle.

To begin understanding the wave particle duality, we can start with looking at photons. To begin understanding such a concept at a high theoretical level, we need to lay down some foundational pillars of understanding. To set the scene we are going to jump around a bit in time. For beginners, let’s jump to the 18-hundreds.

In 1801 Thomas Young set up an experiment where he decided to study photons. Wait, wait wait, first we need to look into both Max Planck, Albert Einstein and maybe also Gilbert Lewis. If we want to be really nitpicky we might also set the scene by going into Isaac Newton and top it all off by looking at it from a philosophical framework.

From the theory of Max Planck, Albert Einstein worked on the principle of photons, which later became attributed to Gilbert Lewis because of his creation of the word from the term, “smallest unit of radiant energy”. In other words, the particles we observe from light have a certain amount of energy, a photon.

These giants again built their work upon the genius himself, Isaac Newton, who studied light in the 1700 century. His early work was a theory where light consists of what he termed “corpuscles”, which is another word for what we today refer to as particles. These corpuscles move in a line with a finite velocity and have kinetic energy, or as they said back in the days, impetus.

IMPETUS: a (1) : a driving force IMPULSE (2) : INCENTIVE, STIMULUS b : stimulation or encouragement resulting in increased activity 2 : the property possessed by a moving body in virtue of its mass and its motion —used of bodies moving suddenly or violently to indicate the origin and intensity of the motion

This corpuscular theory of light was later worked upon by another great mind, René Descartes, before being continued by Chrisitian Huygens.

When we think back at these times, the general zeitgeist, or the way thinkers thought about matter, were mostly philosophical and were “Aristotian” in principle. At the time, they changed their ways of thinking to a more clock-like workings understanding of the universe, where matter and motion dictated the way we understood the universe.

When we think about these concepts it’s great to have in mind the way these scientists thought about concepts. And how and why they thought upon their theories is again decided by their general understanding of the theories that plagued their lifetime.

These thinkers sought the likes of Democritus (from the theory of the atom), and more generally Epicureanism which is a philosophical system that bases its system on atomists and materialism.

Anyways, I digress. Back to the wave-particle duality.
